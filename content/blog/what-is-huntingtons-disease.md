Huntington's Disease is one of the most devastating neurological conditions a family can face. Yet most people have never heard of it — until the day it enters their lives.

This guide is for those people. The ones who just received a diagnosis. The ones whose parent or partner or child tested positive for the gene. The ones who found this page at 2am trying to understand what lies ahead.

## What Is Huntington's Disease?

Huntington's Disease (HD) is a hereditary, progressive brain disorder caused by a mutation in the HTT gene on chromosome 4. It causes the gradual breakdown of nerve cells in the brain — a process that is irreversible and, currently, fatal.

Neurologists often describe HD as experiencing Parkinson's disease, ALS, and Alzheimer's disease simultaneously. Motor control deteriorates. Cognition declines. Psychiatric symptoms — depression, anxiety, impulsivity — often appear before the physical symptoms do.

There is currently no cure.

## Who Gets Huntington's Disease?

HD is inherited in what's called an autosomal dominant pattern. That means:

- If one parent carries the mutated HTT gene, each child has a **50% chance** of inheriting it
- Anyone who inherits the mutation will eventually develop the disease — it is fully penetrant
- HD does not skip generations

Globally, approximately **1 in 10,000 people** are affected. In the United States, around 30,000 people are living with HD and another 200,000 are at risk.

## When Do Symptoms Begin?

Most people with HD develop symptoms between the ages of 30 and 50 — which means the disease strikes during the years when people are raising children, building careers, and caring for aging parents.

The timeline varies, but the disease typically progresses over 10–25 years after symptom onset.

Early symptoms often include:

- Subtle changes in mood, cognition, or coordination
- Irritability, depression, or difficulty with complex tasks
- Small involuntary movements (chorea)

As the disease progresses, movement becomes increasingly uncontrolled. Speaking, swallowing, and walking all deteriorate. Cognitive decline mirrors dementia. In the final stages, total care is required.

## Is There a Test?

Yes. Predictive genetic testing can determine whether someone carries the HTT mutation — before any symptoms appear. This is both a gift and a burden. Some at-risk individuals choose to know. Many do not.

Genetic counseling is strongly recommended before and after testing. The specialists at the **UC Davis Huntington's Disease Center of Excellence** — the clinic NVforHD supports — include dedicated HD genetic counselors who guide families through exactly this decision.

## Can It Be Prevented?

For existing carriers, HD cannot be prevented. But the cycle can be broken.

Through IVF with preimplantation genetic testing (PGT), couples where one partner carries the mutation can have children who are HD-free. This is the work that **HelpCureHD** — NVforHD's 2024 charity partner — funds directly.

Brandon and Rylee Puccini are now expecting a healthy baby, HD-free, because of funds raised at our 2024 golf tournament.

## What Support Exists?

HD families need more than a neurologist. The UC Davis HD Center of Excellence — one of only a handful of specialty HD clinics in the country — provides a full interdisciplinary team:

- Movement disorder neurologists
- HD-specialized psychiatrists
- Genetic counselors
- Social workers
- Physical therapists
- Research coordinators

This clinic serves over **90 families in Northern Nevada and the Sierra Foothills** — families who have no other specialty care within reach. It operates entirely on private donation.
